# Annual Budget 2026-2027

## Scope

This page sets the first 12-month treasury budget for MonkeyWifHat assuming launch in early April 2026.

For planning purposes, this budget covers April 2026 through March 2027.

This is a conservative, lean, token-only accounting budget.

That means the project tracks treasury commitments primarily in token terms, keeps routine spending narrow, and avoids pretending the treasury is more liquid than it really is.

## Budget Period

- reporting period: April 2026 through March 2027
- publication date: before launch
- next planned review date: end of Q2 2026 after the first full operating quarter

## Budget Posture

The Year 1 priority is not maximum spend. The Year 1 priority is establishing repeatable routines, visible outputs, and enough restraint that the project still has flexibility after the first wave of launch activity.

This budget therefore assumes:

- token-only treasury accounting
- conservative and lean spending
- routine community campaigns over one-off spectacle
- launch support and operational precursors funded first
- formal audit or verification budget lines kept visible from the start
- partnerships treated as gated opportunities, not standing entitlement

## Wallets Covered

- creator and community treasury wallet
- operations and security multisig
- team vesting wallet only when a vesting disclosure update is required

## Year 1 Budget Ceiling

The broader tokenomics framework allows planning for up to:

- `20%` of the creator and community treasury bucket in Year 1
- `25%` of the operations and security bucket in Year 1

This budget intentionally plans below those ceilings.

Recommended Year 1 operating target:

- up to `15%` of the creator and community treasury bucket
- up to `18%` of the operations and security bucket

If the project can operate below those levels while maintaining visible output, it should do so.

## Planned Budget By Category

### Creator and Community Treasury

Primary Year 1 objective: build recurring habits that make the project feel alive on a schedule.

Recommended community-budget mix:

- `40%` recurring community campaigns and meme programs
- `20%` creator rewards and contests
- `15%` art, design, and content production
- `5%` collaborations and partner-linked initiatives
- `20%` reserve and contingency

Interpretation:

- recurring campaigns come first because routine matters more than isolated spikes
- creator rewards exist to reinforce the routine, not replace it
- art and content production support the campaign loop with reusable assets
- collaboration spending stays small until partnership standards are clearer
- reserve is deliberately large to protect against overcommitting too early

### Operations and Security

Primary Year 1 objective: support launch properly and keep the operating base credible.

Recommended operations-budget mix:

- `30%` launch and publishing support
- `25%` infrastructure and tooling
- `25%` security, audits, and technical verification
- `10%` moderation and community operations
- `10%` legal, administrative, and emergency reserve

Interpretation:

- launch and publishing support are front-loaded because weak execution damages trust early
- infrastructure and tooling should exist before volume arrives, not after
- security and audit spending is formal, visible, and not treated as an afterthought
- moderation is budgeted, but kept lean until traffic justifies expansion

## Routine Campaign Standard

The project should behave like it has a schedule.

Year 1 campaign spending should favor repeatable loops such as:

- biweekly meme prompts
- monthly creator or community spotlights
- recurring hat-of-the-week or themed remix cycles
- periodic asset-pack refreshes tied to disclosed budgets

Recommended baseline cadence:

- one biweekly meme prompt cycle
- one monthly spotlight cycle
- one visible community prompt or recap attached to each cycle

The exact format can tighten after launch, but the core rule should remain the same: if the community cannot tell when the next beat is coming, the routine is too loose.

## Creator Program Standard

The creator program should stay narrow in Year 1.

Recommended uses:

- small disclosed bounties tied to asset packs or themed content
- spotlight rewards for visible community contributions
- limited contest rewards attached to recurring campaign loops

What should wait until later:

- open-ended grant systems
- large standing creator funds
- complicated application processes pretending to be a foundation

## Partnership Standard

Partnership activity in Year 1 should be selective and documented.

The working model is simple:

- partnership allocations are not automatic
- any partner-linked token distribution should have a named purpose and visible output
- collaboration budget should remain small until partnership rules are mature
- sponsor or brand participation should be framed as a disclosed promotional relationship, not as implied asset backing

In plain terms, the project may use token allocations as part of branded participation or collaborative campaigns, but it should not talk as if the token is redeemed into hard value by rhetoric alone.

## Token-Only Accounting Standard

For Year 1, the treasury should be reported primarily in token units.

That means:

- budgets are approved in token terms first
- the project does not assume stable conversion value in advance
- community commitments should not depend on optimistic price assumptions
- treasury reports should state clearly when a plan is token-denominated versus when a conversion actually happened

If outside support or sponsorship is pursued, the public explanation should stay simple:

- the sponsor is participating in a disclosed campaign or collaboration
- the sponsor relationship does not rewrite the token's purpose
- any token allocation tied to that relationship is disclosed as a promotional or partnership budget item

## Treasury Conversion Standard

The treasury should not rely on routine token selling to fund basic operations.

Year 1 default:

- no routine treasury sales assumed in the base budget
- no community-program promise should depend on future treasury conversion
- any conversion from tokens into operating funds should be treated as an exception event of last resort
- exception conversions require a public note stating the reason, amount, wallet used, and effect on runway

This keeps the project from quietly turning treasury management into hidden supply pressure.

## Liquidity Seeding Standard

Liquidity should be seeded as part of launch structure, not created later by draining the treasury.

Year 1 default:

- initial liquidity comes from the `72%` public and liquidity bucket, not from the creator treasury
- the amount committed to initial liquidity should be disclosed before launch
- the wallet used for liquidity seeding should be published with the rest of the wallet map
- the default seeding method is to burn the LP position after launch creation
- if the LP position cannot be burned for a technical reason, the deviation should be explained before launch rather than after it

This creates a cleaner trustless posture because the market-facing pool is seeded upfront and the treasury does not need to quietly sell its own budget to keep trading alive.

## Trustless Default

If the goal is to outlast, the clean default is:

- seed liquidity at launch from the public and liquidity allocation
- publish the liquidity wallet and burned-LP method
- run the treasury in token terms without assuming routine conversions
- reduce program scope before selling treasury-held tokens

That is stricter than most token launches, but it is also easier to explain and verify.

## Runway Guardrail

This first annual budget should preserve the longer-term treasury plan, not consume it.

Year 1 should therefore maintain:

- a minimum planned runway of `18 months`
- a preferred downside runway of `30 months`
- a standing willingness to slow campaign scope before approving any treasury conversion activity

If the treasury starts feeling tight, the first move is to reduce scope and frequency, not to improvise a new narrative.

## Year 1 Visible Outputs

By the end of the first 12 months, treasury spending should point to visible results such as:

- a recognizable recurring campaign rhythm
- documented creator and contest payouts
- refreshed asset packs and content support
- a launch executed with published disclosures intact
- formal security or verification work completed and reported

If spending does not produce one of those outputs, it should face a high burden of explanation.

## Review Standard

This annual budget should be reviewed quarterly against three questions:

- is routine actually being maintained
- is spending staying below the conservative Year 1 target
- is the runway still strong enough to avoid reactive treasury behavior

If the answer to any of those turns negative, the budget should tighten before anything is expanded.
